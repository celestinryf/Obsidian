		- Working on the workspaces feature for the document management system for UW internship. In the database, I struggled with understanding tables as maps for working with personalized workspaces for users
	- Answer

		- At my UW internship, I was building a workspaces feature for our internal document management system — the idea was that each faculty member would have personalized workspaces where they could organize and share worksheets. I hit a wall on the database design. I needed to model workspaces in a way where users could have their own views of shared documents, and I kept trying to force a traditional relational structure that wasn't working. The core problem was understanding how to use junction tables essentially as maps — mapping users to workspaces to documents with different permission levels — and I hadn't designed anything like that before. 
		- 
		- The added pressure was that this was on a tight sprint deadline, and the rest of the team was heads-down on other features, so I couldn't just hand it off or wait for someone to walk me through it. So I did what I always do when I'm stuck — I broke the problem down. I stepped away from the code entirely and white boarded the data relationships on paper. I mapped out every entity and asked myself what actually needs to relate to what. Then I went and read documentation on how other systems model multi-tenant workspaces and compared three different approaches. Once I saw the pattern — that the junction table was essentially a key-value map linking users to scoped resources — it clicked, and I was able to implement it and write the JUnit tests to validate the access logic.
		- 
		- My instinct now when I hit something unfamiliar is to stop coding, go back to first principles, research how others have solved it, and then come back with a plan rather than brute-forcing it.
